Types of Design: Fixed

Fixed | Fluid | Adaptive | Responsive

Fixed design is how we originally started developing web pages. Most content was static, there wasn't much dynamic content (back in the days of dial-up Internet, some people didn't even download images by default, because they were so slow!)

Fixed design used absolute size measurements, such as points (pt), pixels (px), and even centimetres (cm), millimetres (mm) and inches (in)! Back then, people didn't have small mobile devices - everyone used a large laptop or desktop. All the screens we viewed things on were very large, but they had an aspect ratio of 4:3, so they were almost square. Eventually, wider screens became popular, and people had more width to play with when designing web pages.

With a fixed layout, your page didn't respond to changes in viewport size, and certainly not to changes in orientation! It's not like you could pick up your desktop monitor or laptop and tilt it to change the orientation like we can today with smaller devices! Thinking of that actually makes me laugh a little, and maybe you too, if you're over 35 :D

This page uses a fixed layout, and I even designed it how I would have laid it out back in the days when people started using wider screens: I design for the 4:3 screen and add some decorative background for people that had a wider screen. We didn't want to put content in that extra space, because then people with the 4:3 screens wouldn't see it!

Try viewing this page in your browser the way you would normally view it on a wide screen, then try resizing your screen to approximate a 4:3 aspect ratio. You can also try resizing your screen to the size of a tablet and/or smart phone: you'll notice that it's near impossible to use the page!

Feel free to check the external CSS file I used for this page. Notice that I even used the old style of laying out the page in HTML - using DIV elements instead of using the HTML5 semantic elements like HEADER, FOOTER, MAIN, etc.
